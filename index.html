Hearing loss affects millions worldwide and remains incurable. Unlike other vertebrates such as birds and fish, mammals cannot regenerate sensory hair cells in the inner ear once they are lost. Our long-term goal is to develop biological treatments to restore sound sensation in patients by unlocking the regenerative potential that exists in non-mammalian species.

We aim to discover—in detail—the molecular mechanisms and signaling pathways by which non-mammalian species naturally regenerate lost sensory hair cells in their inner ears. By understanding these fundamental biological processes, we can identify therapeutic targets and develop interventions that could restore this regenerative capacity in humans.

In parallel, we are developing a comprehensive platform in adult mice that allows us to systematically evaluate and screen for drugs, small molecules, and genetic manipulations that lead to proliferative hair cell regeneration in the deaf cochlea. This translational approach combines basic science discoveries with practical therapeutic development, bringing us closer to clinical applications that could transform the lives of millions affected by hearing loss.

Hubert Löwenheim, MD PhD
Principal Investigator

Professor Löwenheim leads groundbreaking research in auditory neuroscience, focusing on the molecular mechanisms of hair cell regeneration and the development of novel therapeutic approaches for hearing restoration. His work bridges fundamental biological research with clinical applications, advancing our understanding of inner ear biology and developing innovative treatments for hearing disorders.

His research encompasses multiple cutting-edge areas including gene therapy for inherited deafness, advanced imaging techniques for cochlear implant planning, digital therapeutic interventions for vestibular disorders, and the investigation of regenerative pathways that could restore hearing in mammals. Through systematic drug screening platforms and translational research, his laboratory is pioneering new approaches to overcome one of medicine's most challenging problems: the irreversible loss of sensory hair cells in the inner ear.

Research Focus

Hair Cell Regeneration

Investigating the molecular mechanisms and signaling pathways that enable non-mammalian species to naturally regenerate sensory hair cells. Our research identifies key transcription factors, growth factors, and cellular processes that could be therapeutically targeted to restore regenerative capacity in mammalian inner ears.

Gene Therapy Development

Developing advanced gene therapy approaches including DB-OTO dual adeno-associated virus therapy for inherited deafness caused by otoferlin deficiency. This work explores novel delivery methods and genetic constructs to restore protein function in cochlear hair cells and synaptic transmission.

Drug Screening Platforms

Building comprehensive platforms for systematic evaluation and high-throughput screening of pharmaceutical compounds, small molecules, and biological agents that promote proliferative hair cell regeneration in adult mammalian cochleae, bridging discovery research with therapeutic development.

Clinical Translation

Advancing cochlear implant technology through innovative preoperative diagnostic techniques, including photon-counting detector CT with deep learning-supported denoising for dose reduction, enabling more precise surgical planning and improved patient outcomes while minimizing radiation exposure.

Vestibular & Phonation Research

Developing digital therapeutic interventions for vestibular vertigo through evidence-based protocols and investigating novel non-contact laryngeal magnetomyography techniques for phonation differentiation, expanding our understanding of auditory-vestibular system function and disorders.

Translational Therapeutic Development

Creating biological interventions and regenerative medicine strategies to restore auditory function in patients with sensorineural hearing loss. Our translational research pipeline moves discoveries from bench to bedside, addressing the clinical needs of millions affected by hearing disorders worldwide.
